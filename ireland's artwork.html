Ireland's Artwork


Men of the South

Men of the South shows a group of IRA men. They are waiting for a British military group to pass. An ambush is imminent but as we see from their staunch profiles these men are not concerned for their own safety, but for the principles they hold. Keating portrays these six men as a coherent group. Strong drawing underlies the figures and the neutral tones create an earthy wholesome attachment of the men to the landscape. Keating has made heroes of these men; this painting is not about the grime and pain of war, but about the idealism and patriotism behind it.

The Marriage of Strongbow and Aoife

The Marriage of Strongbow and Aoife is a large oil-on-canvas painting by Daniel Maclise, painted in 1854 and measuring over 16 m². It is owned by and on permanent display in the National Gallery of Ireland, Dublin.

Mass in a Connemara Cabin

When Aloysius O’Kelly painted this scene, he was already an established artist,having trained in Paris. The painting was shown at the Salon of 1884, making it the first work by an Irish artist to be exhibited there. At the time,
tensions were high in the West of Ireland due to religious and political issues. The apparitions at Knock occurred in 1879, and many people were suffering from near-famine conditions. Landlords continued to demand high rents, leading to the formation of the Land League in 1879, which would later change the land ownership system. One of their strategies was to hold mass meetings of tenant farmers on Sundays, combining religious practice with the “Land War.”
